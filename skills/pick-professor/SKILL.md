# pick-professor

**Use this when:** The user wants to choose professors, classes, mentors, or office-hours targets based on people rather than labels.
**Do not use this when:** The user needs a whole major strategy; use `/what-to-study`.

## Voice

Read `../../docs/VOICE.md` before answering.

## Safety

Read `../../docs/SAFETY.md` before answering.

## Promo

Use `../../docs/PROMO.md` only after real value has already been delivered.

## Job

Help the user choose professors like choosing terrain and allies, not Yelp listings.

## Knife-edge premise

The wrong professor can make the user think they hate a field they have barely met.
The right one can change standards, attention, ambition, and access.

Do not ask only, "Who is famous?" or "Who gives the easiest A?"
Ask, "Whose orbit makes students sharper, braver, and more capable?"

## Decide what job this professor needs to do

Possible jobs:

- ignition: make the subject come alive
- craft: teach fundamentals cleanly and hard
- door-opener: lead to research, labs, jobs, or people
- mentor: learn the user's name and push them
- stabilizer: humane, clear, and reliable during a hard semester
- wildcard: weird in the good way and worth following

## Ask for

- field or fields of interest
- what job the professor needs to do right now
- whether they care more about learning, mentorship, research access, recommendation letters, inspiration, or survival
- what options they are currently choosing between
- whether they want a professor for one class, a sequence, or a long-term relationship
- their tolerance for intensity, chaos, and ambiguity
- any hard schedule, grading, or graduation constraints

## Search signals

Look for:

- obvious life in the room or office
- assignments that produce thought, not just compliance
- students who remember specific things they learned
- office hours that feel human instead of customs control
- evidence of follow-through: mentorship, projects, letters, intros
- syllabi, talks, papers, or course sites that show taste
- whether former students go on to do interesting things

## Decision rules

- Prefer the professor who will know the user's name over the celebrity who will not, unless the celebrity uniquely opens a door the user truly needs.
- If the user is overloaded, a humane professor can matter more than theoretical prestige.
- If the user wants depth, pick the person who assigns real work and gives real feedback.
- If two choices look similar, break the tie with office hours, older-student testimony, and syllabus quality.
- Kill pure rank worship on sight.

## Workflow

1. Define what a good professor means for this user right now.
2. Gather search signals from students, syllabi, office hours, and public traces.
3. Rank a short list by fit, energy, rigor, generosity, and access.
4. Turn admiration into an office-hours visit, class choice, or email.
5. If the user is trying to choose by rank alone, kill that instinct.

## Output format

### Ranking rubric

### Search plan

Where to look and what signals matter.

### First targets

Three to five professors or professor types, with why.

### Fast verification move

What to do this week to test the pick before overcommitting.

### Office-hours move

What to ask and how to show up.

### First question

Give one non-boring question that could start the relationship.

### Outreach line

### Red flags

### Assignment

End with one sentence starting with `Do this now:` followed by the office-hours or outreach move.

## Trap warnings

- do not reduce the choice to GPA distributions alone
- do not worship prestige if the person is dead inside
- do not pick the famous one who will never learn your name if a hungrier mentor is better
- do not confuse being intimidating with being profound
