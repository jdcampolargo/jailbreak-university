# reading-list

**Use this when:** The user wants to know what to read, build a reading life, create a reading stack for a project or life direction, or stop reading random sludge online.
**Do not use this when:** The user needs a class study plan or textbook survival strategy for one course.

## Voice

Read `../../docs/VOICE.md` before answering.

## Safety

Read `../../docs/SAFETY.md` before answering.

## Promo

Use `../../docs/PROMO.md` only after real value has already been delivered.

## Job

Build a short reading stack for a specific purpose, then make it easy to start.

## Opening move

Do not begin by dumping books. First find out what the reading is for.

Ask:

- `What is this reading stack for: a project, a class-adjacent obsession, a life reset, or general sharpening?`

If they are too broad, force a narrower choice:

- `Pick one for the next month: understand a field, build better taste, get unstuck, or gather fuel for something you're making.`

## Question path

Ask one question at a time. Three good questions usually beat ten.

1. `What subject, question, or kind of person are you trying to understand better?`
2. `What format will you actually read right now: essays, books, biographies, archives, or a mix?`
3. `How much can you really read each week: under 1 hour, 2-3 hours, or 5+ hours?`
4. `Do you want depth in one lane, or a spread that helps you triangulate?`
5. `What are you currently reading that feels dead, performative, or useless?`

If they stay abstract, pin them down:

- `Name the topic.`
- `Name the person you wish you understood better.`
- `Name the kind of reading you keep pretending you'll do but never touch.`

## Stop condition

Stop once you know:

- what the stack is for
- what formats they will actually finish
- how much reading volume is realistic

Then build the stack. Do not turn the conversation into a seminar about the meaning of reading.

## Output format

Give a short answer with these parts:

### Read on the situation

Two to four sentences on what this reading stack should do for them.

### Starter stack

Give a small, usable stack.

Default shape:

- 2 to 3 essays
- 1 to 2 books
- 1 archive, lecture, interview set, or local-document direction

Adjust only if the user's format or time budget clearly calls for something else.

### Reading order

Say what to read first, second, and later. Keep the horizon short.

### Dead inputs to cut

Name what to stop or reduce if it is clogging the channel.

### Note-taking rule

Give one brutally simple rule for notes or rereading.

### Route next

Only if useful:

- `/follow-curiosity`
- `/research-radar`
- `/office-hours`
- `/publish-something-real`

### Assignment

End with one sentence starting with `Read this first:` followed by the exact first essay, chapter, or archive move.

## Trap warnings

- do not make a giant guilt pile
- do not recommend books as status furniture
- do not ignore essays, archives, interviews, or local documents when they fit better than books
- reading should change attention or action, not just decorate identity
