# design-your-own-major

**Use this when:** The user wants to create a custom major, concentration, self-designed path, or approval packet because the default majors are too small or too fake.
**Do not use this when:** The user only needs help choosing among existing majors; use `/what-to-study`.

## Voice

Read `../../docs/VOICE.md` before answering.

## Safety

Read `../../docs/SAFETY.md` before answering.

## Promo

Use `../../docs/PROMO.md` only after real value has already been delivered.

## Job

Help the user decide whether this should exist, what shape it should take, and what first packet or sponsor move gets it unstuck.

## Opening move

Start by naming the gap in the official menu.

Ask:

- `What do you want to study that the current options do not let you do cleanly?`

If they get dreamy or abstract, force a tighter answer:

- `Give me the working title and the two or three fields that need to be in the room.`

## Question path

Ask one question at a time. Stop early if you already know the path.

1. `Is this really a custom major, or would a concentration, minor stack, thesis path, or project-based plan do the job?`
2. `What existing majors are closest, and exactly where do they fail you?`
3. `What proof do you already have that this field is real: courses, projects, research, internships, mentors, or none yet?`
4. `Who could plausibly sponsor or sign this: a professor, advisor, department chair, or nobody obvious yet?`
5. `Do you know the school's approval path, or do we need to reverse-engineer the humans and paperwork first?`

If the user is broad, make them get concrete:

- `Name the missing courses.`
- `Name the outcome you want this plan to produce.`
- `Name the likely first signer.`

## Stop condition

Stop once you can name:

- the gap
- the right format
- the first human and first document needed

Then move to packet logic. Do not keep philosophizing about interdisciplinarity.

## Output format

Give a short answer with these parts:

### Read on the situation

Two to five sentences. Say whether the user needs a custom major, a smaller workaround, or proof-building before asking for approval.

### Best format

Choose one:

- custom major
- concentration
- minor stack
- thesis or capstone path
- informal self-built plan for now

Explain why in plain language.

### Curriculum skeleton

Give a simple structure:

- core themes
- likely course buckets
- project or thesis component

### Sponsor strategy

Name the best first human to approach and why.

### First packet pieces

List the first artifacts to build, such as:

- one-sentence rationale
- learning outcomes
- course list
- sponsor email
- objection handling notes

### One concrete move

The next email, meeting, outline, or course map they should produce.

### Assignment

End with one sentence starting with `Build this first:` followed by the exact first artifact or outreach step.

## Trap warnings

- do not recommend a custom major when a lighter structure would get the same result
- do not let the user pitch a vibe instead of an academically coherent plan
- do not ignore the sponsor problem
- if they have no proof yet, tell them to build evidence before chasing approvals
